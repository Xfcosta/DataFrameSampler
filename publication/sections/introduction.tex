\section{Introduction}

Tabular datasets remain a central representation in business analytics, health
informatics, scientific computing, and applied machine learning, and many
software workflows require example tables that preserve at least some schema and
distributional properties of an existing source table. However, direct reuse of
source records can be infeasible even when the intended use is modest, because
governance rules, licensing constraints, privacy concerns, or operational
separation may prevent developers from placing real rows in tests, dashboards,
training material, or notebooks. The resulting need is therefore not identical
to the classical problem of producing a high-fidelity or formally private
synthetic release. It is a narrower constructive problem: given a mixed-type
dataframe, produce example rows that are useful enough to support low-stakes
workflows while keeping the modelling assumptions visible.

DataFrameSampler targets this pragmatic setting. In principle, one could use a
large generative model, a disclosure-control system, or a task-specific
oversampler, but these alternatives often introduce additional dependencies,
implicit objectives, or claims that are stronger than the use case requires. We
therefore make a conservative design choice: the method should be small,
inspectable, and explicit about its failure modes, even if this excludes formal
optimality and formal privacy guarantees. In other words, the paper is not a
claim of state-of-the-art synthetic-data fidelity; it is an argument that a
structured and auditable heuristic can be useful when the target workflow is
example generation rather than data release.

Version 2.0 uses a supervised latent representation throughout. Non-binary
numeric columns are median-imputed and standardized, while every categorical
column, including booleans and numeric two-valued columns such as $0/1$, is
assigned its own NCA block learned from the remaining columns. Generated latent
points are then mapped back to the original schema by inverse numeric scaling
and probabilistic categorical classification. This construction is deliberately
modular: the encoder defines local supervised geometry, the transport step
constructs candidate latent rows, and the decoder expresses uncertainty over
observed categorical labels.

The contributions are fourfold. First, we provide a direct
\texttt{DataFrameSampler} estimator with \texttt{fit}, \texttt{transform},
\texttt{inverse\_transform}, and \texttt{generate}. Second, we define an
iterative per-categorical-column NCA latent construction for mixed-type
dataframes. Third, we use a local mutual-neighbour transport rule in the learned
space as an inspectable generation mechanism. Fourth, we pair the method with
claim-specific diagnostics for distributional similarity, downstream utility,
neighbour-distance behaviour, NCA-block mechanism evidence, decoder
calibration, and extrapolative manifold consistency.
